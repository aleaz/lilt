\begin{abstract}
Scholarly articles mix running prose with equations, floats, and citations.
This short note sketches an integrity-first localization workflow in which a
local \toolname{} masks non-translatable tokens before machine translation and
restores them afterward. The constructions below are deliberately minimal, yet
representative of everyday scientific publishing practice.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Authors who maintain bilingual technical manuscripts need more than fluent
prose: labels, cite keys, and graphics paths must survive localization.
Operators therefore treat structure as a first-class constraint and keep product
names such as \toolname{} out of the editable translation span via protected
terms. Editorial links may appear as
\href{https://www.latex-project.org/}{project documentation}, while emphasis
that must not lose its color argument can use
\textcolor{blue}{stable highlight tokens}.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

Masked display mathematics remains byte-stable across languages. A toy energy
relation is shown in~\cref{eq:energy}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:energy}
E = mc^{2}
\end{equation}

A second display identity used for cross-checks is

\begin{align}
\label{eq:sum}
a + b &= c \\
c - a &= b
\end{align}

Physical quantities in the narrative use \texttt{siunitx}, for example a sample
length of \SI{12.5}{\milli\metre} and a rate expressed with unit
\si{\metre\per\second}. Inline fragments such as $x^{2}+y^{2}$ appear only as
supporting context.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Prior notes argue that integrity should precede fluency
\cite{integrity2024}. Incremental memory-backed workflows are summarized
by~\cite[see][§2]{workflow2025}, while unit-aware typesetting practice is
surveyed in~\cite{units2023}. As \enquote{structure first} operators often
remark, the reader should never notice repaired markup---only improved prose.
